## The Amiga Was Always Ahead

The Commodore Amiga arrived in 1985 with hardware that competitors
would not match for nearly a decade:

```
Amiga 1000 (1985):
  Custom co-processor chips for audio, video, DMA
  4096 colour palette (HAM mode)
  4-channel hardware audio DMA
  Hardware sprites
  Preemptive multitasking OS
  All at a time when PCs ran DOS in 16 colours
```

The philosophy of dedicated co-processors handling
audio, video and DMA independently of the CPU --
leaving the CPU free for actual work --
is exactly the architecture Morosa-1200 revives
with the CM4 co-processor model.

The Amiga did it in hardware in 1985.
Morosa-1200 does it with ARM in 2026.

## The Video Toaster -- Ray Tracing Before Anyone

NewTek Video Toaster launched in 1990 for the Amiga 2000.
Price: $1595 for the card plus an Amiga 2000.
Total system cost: under $3000.

Competing Silicon Graphics workstations: $50,000+

The Video Toaster bundled LightWave 3D and delivered:
- Full ray tracing engine (reflections, refractions, shadows)
- Real-time video switching and effects
- Character generation
- Chroma key compositing
- Broadcast quality output

This was ray tracing on consumer hardware in 1990.
id Software would not release Quake (real-time 3D) until 1996.
Pixar's Toy Story (first full CGI film) was 1995.
The Video Toaster was doing ray traced 3D rendering
for broadcast television five years before either.

## The Commodore Betrayal

The Video Toaster was the Amiga's killer app.
It single-handedly kept the platform relevant
into the mid-1990s.

NewTek and Commodore had a difficult relationship.
Commodore never properly marketed the Video Toaster.
Commodore went bankrupt in April 1994.

At the moment the Amiga was producing Babylon 5,
Jurassic Park and Star Trek Voyager --
at the moment it was the most creatively powerful
personal computer ever made --
Commodore collapsed.

The platform that invented affordable ray tracing,
that put broadcast CGI within reach of small studios,
that ran preemptive multitasking in 1985,
was abandoned by its manufacturer at its creative peak.

## The Morosa-1200 Connection

Morosa-1200 is not nostalgia. It is continuation.

The Video Toaster proved in 1990 that:
- Co-processor architecture works (custom chips + CPU)
- Affordable hardware can do what expensive hardware does
- The Amiga platform was architecturally correct

Morosa-1200 in 2026:
- Same co-processor philosophy (68030 + CM4 + GPU)
- Real AGA silicon preserved
- Modern GPU via CM4: RX 5900 XT with hardware ray tracing
- LightWave still exists and still runs
- Ray traced LightWave previews in real time
  on a machine whose architecture descends
  directly from the Video Toaster era

```
1990: Video Toaster does ray tracing on Amiga
      $3000 vs $50,000 SGI workstation

2026: Morosa-1200 does real-time ray tracing
      RX 5900 XT via CM4 co-processor
      On a machine running AmigaOS 3.x
      On real AGA silicon
      For the cost of parts and a PCB
```

The Video Toaster started something.
Morosa-1200 finishes it.

## Foundation Imaging

Foundation Imaging was the production company
that made the Amiga Video Toaster famous.
They produced CGI for:
- Babylon 5 (seasons 1-4)
- Star Trek: Voyager
- Space: Above and Beyond
- seaQuest DSV
- Star Trek: Enterprise (season 1, final production)

The company was dissolved in 2002 after Star Trek Enterprise.
Assets sold at public auction December 17, 2002.

Their pioneering work on Babylon 5 made LightWave 3D
the industry standard for television VFX throughout the 1990s.
Every TV show with CGI space effects in the 1990s
was running on tools that trace back to
an Amiga Video Toaster in a small studio in California.
